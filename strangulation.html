Critical Knowledge for First Responders

Strangulation can cause death up to 36 hours after the incident. Many victims show no visible injuries initially but may have serious internal damage. This is one of the strongest predictors of future homicide in IPV cases.

ü©∫ Immediate Signs (0-24 hours)

Raspy or Hoarse Voice

Voice changes from swelling of vocal cords and throat tissues. Often the first and most obvious sign.

Appears: Immediately

Difficulty Swallowing

Throat pain, feeling like something is stuck, unable to swallow saliva properly.

Appears: Immediately

Neck Pain or Stiffness

Tenderness around throat area, difficulty moving neck, muscle soreness.

Appears: Immediately

Scratches or Fingernail Marks

Linear marks on neck from victim trying to remove hands, or from attacker's fingernails.

Appears: Immediately

Red Eyes (Petechiae)

Small red spots in eyes from burst blood vessels. May also appear on face, eyelids, or neck.

Appears: Within hours

Swollen Tongue or Lips

Tongue may be bitten during strangulation, lips may be swollen from lack of oxygen.

Appears: Within hours

‚è∞ Delayed Signs (24-72 hours)

Bruising Patterns

Finger-shaped bruises on neck, often more visible 24-48 hours later. May be on front, sides, or back of neck.

Appears: 1-2 days later

Headaches

Severe headaches from oxygen deprivation to the brain, different from normal headaches.

Appears: Hours to days

Memory Problems

Confusion, difficulty remembering the incident, concentration problems from brain oxygen loss.

Appears: Hours to days

Vision Changes

Blurred vision, seeing spots, light sensitivity from pressure on blood vessels.

Appears: Hours to days

Breathing Difficulties

Shortness of breath, feeling like throat is closing, difficulty getting enough air.

Can persist for days

Involuntary Urination

Loss of bladder control during strangulation due to lack of oxygen to brain.

During/immediately after

üß† Neurological Impact

What Happens During Strangulation:

4-10 seconds: Loss of consciousness begins
10-15 seconds: Complete unconsciousness
1-3 minutes: Brain damage may begin
3-5 minutes: Serious brain injury likely
5+ minutes: Death or permanent brain damage

Hidden Danger

Victims can appear normal initially but deteriorate rapidly. Internal injuries to blood vessels, airways, or brain may not show symptoms for hours. Always recommend immediate medical evaluation.

Documentation Checklist

  • Photograph all visible marks on neck (front, sides, back)
  • Document voice changes - record if possible
  • Note any red spots in eyes or on face
  • Record victim's description of breathing difficulties
  • Document any loss of consciousness reported
  • Note scratches or defensive wounds on victim's hands
  • Record exact words used to describe the incident
  • Document any involuntary urination or defecation
  • Arrange immediate medical examination
  • Follow up in 24-48 hours for delayed bruising

Remember: Strangulation is Attempted Murder

Even without visible injuries, strangulation is a serious felony and strong predictor of future homicide. Take every case seriously and ensure immediate medical evaluation.
